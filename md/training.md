## Offseason Phase Structure

Three distinct phases — each has different training volume, nutrition, and PED directives:

| Phase | Training Volume | Nutrition | PEDs |
|-------|----------------|-----------|------|
| **Push (Muscle Building)** | Progressively INCREASES each week | Progressively increasing calories | Progressively increase |
| **Holding (Transition)** | Deliberately REDUCED to recoverable baseline | Near maintenance | Minimum / Cruise |
| **Mini-Cut (Fat Loss)** | Held HIGH for muscle retention | Progressively decreasing | Minimum or start of next blast |

**Body composition triggers (males):**
- Start Bulk phase if: BF ~8–12%
- Start Mini-Cut if: BF reaches ~15–17%

**Why a Holding Phase is required (not just Push → Mini-Cut):**
- New muscle tissue needs time to "hold" — settling point theory; cutting too soon loses it
- Both Push and Fat Loss phases accumulate high fatigue — need a reset
- Allows health markers, hormones, and psychology to recover
- Accounts for life events (travel, illness, stress)

**Sample annual layout (intermediate/advanced):**
- Post-show → Active rest → Intro week → Volume block baseline → Deload
- Block 1 (Blast): Push 14 wks (High vol → taper → High vol → deload) → Holding 8 wks (Lower vol + Cruise)
- Block 2 (Blast): Mini-Cut 5 wks (Moderate vol) → Holding 1 wk → Push 8 wks → Holding 8 wks

## Cut vs. Bulk Training: The Key Differences

### Offseason Push (Bulk)
- Volume increases each mesocycle in line with recovering capacity
- Compound movements prioritized (deadlifts, heavy rows, pressing)
- Full RPE range; metabolic techniques (supersets, rest-pause) allowed when recovered
- 2x/week per muscle group minimum

### Contest Prep — Early (16+ weeks out)
- Normal program structure
- Full compound movements still appropriate
- Standard volume; full rest periods
- Begin shifting to 2–3x/week per muscle group (away from bro splits)

### Contest Prep — Mid (8–15 weeks out)
- Begin reducing exercise fatigue demand
- Swap high-fatigue compounds for machine variations
  - Heavy deadlifts → lighter deadlifts or good mornings (Eric's preference — avoids heavy deadlifts)
- Maintain stimulus, reduce systemic fatigue

### Contest Prep — Late (<8 weeks out)
- Minimum work sets per session
- Remove ALL metabolic techniques: no supersets, rest-pause, forced reps, dropsets
- Shift to lowest-fatigue exercise variants (back extensions instead of deadlifts)
- Split volume across more sessions with shorter duration
  - Example: PPL (3 days) → Upper/Lower (4 days) or Push/Pull/Delt+Arms/Legs (4 days)
- Fight to maintain load and reps on remaining exercises — autoregulate rather than drop weight

**Why bro splits fail during prep:**
- Deficit impairs recovery capacity → too much volume in one session exceeds what you can recover from
- 7-day gap between re-stimulating the same muscle causes adaptation to regress
- Modern approach: 2–3x/week per muscle group at lower volume per session

## Current Program Structure (J3U Mens Physique Template)

**Split options (all run on 7-day cycle):**
- General: Chest-focused Push / Pull / Legs / Off / Shoulder-focused Push / Pull 2 / Off
- Chest-focused, Shoulder-focused, Back-focused, Arm-focused variants available

**Work set standard:**
- All listed sets are WORK sets only (no warmup counted)
- 0–2 RIR on all work sets
- Progress load when you can do 1 rep above the top of the target rep range

**Sample session volumes:**
- Push (chest focus): 6 exercises, ~15–19 work sets, 8–20 reps
- Pull: 6 exercises, ~16 sets, 8–15 reps
- Push 2 / Pull 2: 6 exercises, ~18 sets, 8–20 reps
- Shoulders + Back: 6 exercises, ~18 sets, 8–15 reps

**Abs (2–3x/week):**
- Crunch variation (stretch + contract)
- Plank variation (bracing capacity)
- Rotational/mobility work
- Vacuum upon waking — daily
- Do NOT do abs before heavy leg days or at the start of a session

**Calves:**
- Straight-leg calf raise + seated calf raise + tibialis raise
- Do NOT start leg days with calves
- Pairs well with upper body days

## Hypertrophy Principles (Applied)

**Core principle:** Mechanical tension drives hypertrophy — internal fiber tension, not external load. Works across 30–85% 1RM as long as proximity to failure is sufficient.

**Volume follows an inverse-U curve:**
- Too few sets = atrophy
- Too many sets = atrophy
- Optimal = peak hypertrophy
- Implication: excessive volume during prep actually impairs muscle retention

**Exercise selection criteria (in priority order):**
1. Your movement abilities (mobility vs. dysfunction)
2. Trains the intended muscle (alignment, ROM, bracing, mind-muscle)
3. Strength/resistance profile (lengthened position preferred)
4. Stimulus-to-fatigue ratio
5. Load-ability
6. Time efficiency
7. Intra-session and weekly variation
8. Sustainability

**Why a muscle stays weak (root cause checklist):**
- Poor activation — mobility or movement dysfunction
- Poor stimulus — wrong exercise, poor form/tempo, bad joint alignment, insufficient effort, wrong set count
- Too much fatigue — poor exercise order (e.g., deadlift before rows), excessive warmups, insufficient rest

**Rest periods:** Full rest between work sets (2–4 min for compounds). Insufficient rest = reduced quality, not increased stimulus.

## Training During Diet Break / Deload

- Cut work sets by 50%
- Same exercises, same loads
- Train to 1 RIR (not failure)
- Cardio stays the same unless fatigue is very high
- Duration: 7–14 days alongside calorie reset to maintenance
