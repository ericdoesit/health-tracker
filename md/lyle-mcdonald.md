# Lyle McDonald (bodyrecomposition.com) — Research Summary & Comparison
*Source: bodyrecomposition.com — academic/evidence-based framework*

---

## Core Positions by Topic

### Protein

**Daily targets:**
- Strength athletes in deficit: 1.4–1.5 g/lb LBM
- At extreme leanness (<10% BF): up to 1.5 g/lb LBM
- Muscle gain / maintenance: 1.1–1.4 g/lb LBM

**Per-meal limits (rejects the 43–54g MPS ceiling):**
MPS studies only measure skeletal muscle protein synthesis — they ignore muscle protein breakdown, connective tissue, and organ tissue (50–60% of LBM). There is no hard per-meal ceiling in real-world conditions. More protein in a meal = more whole-body anabolism.

**Meal frequency:** 4–6 meals every 3–5 hours. Eating more often than every 3 hours causes amino acid desensitization. Three meals outperformed six meals for lean mass in one study. Fluctuating amino acid levels matter more than constant elevation.

**Post-workout window:** Largely irrelevant if you ate within 4 hours of training. Pre-workout protein may be superior to post-workout.

---

### Fat Loss: Deficit, Rate, and Muscle Retention

**Maintenance estimate:** Men: 16 cal/lb body weight (~2,880 cal at 180 lbs).

**Three deficit tiers:**
- Small (10–15% below): ~13–15 cal/lb; 0.5–1 lb/week loss. Best for lean athletes.
- Moderate (20–25% below): ~12 cal/lb; 1–1.5 lbs/week. Best for most.
- Large (25%+): ~8–11 cal/lb; 2–3 lbs/week. Short-term only (2–4 weeks max); must curtail training.

**Rate of loss:** 1–1.5 lbs/week is optimal for trained individuals. 2–4 week bursts of rapid loss are acceptable as a jump-start. Getting to single digits: accept 0.5 lbs/week.

**Muscle retention at low BF:** At sub-10% BF, muscle loss ratio approaches 1 lb muscle per 3 lbs total weight. Solutions: high protein (1.4–1.5 g/lb LBM), maintaining training intensity, strategic refeeds.

**Adjust by:** Track every 2–3 weeks (not weekly — water masks true fat loss). Adjust in 10% increments. Use gym performance as secondary signal.

**The cortisol paradox:** "You can either cut calories hard OR do large amounts of activity. But you can't do both." Combining very low calories with heavy cardio creates chronic cortisol elevation → water retention (masking fat loss), leptin resistance, metabolic suppression, stalled progress. This is the most common prep mistake.

### Carbohydrates and Fat

**Carb minimums:**
- 50g/day: limits protein catabolism in ketosis
- 100–120g/day: avoids ketosis entirely
- 120–150g/day: minimum for thyroid/leptin hormonal recovery during diet breaks

**Why carbs matter:** Leptin is carbohydrate-responsive in the short term (2–3 day window) — only dietary carbohydrate raises leptin; dietary fat does not. Thyroid T4-to-T3 conversion in the liver is acutely sensitive to carb intake. High-intensity training cannot be sustained without adequate carbs.

**Dietary fat floor:** 20–25% of total calories for hormonal function and nutrient absorption.

**Fish oil:** 1.8–3.0 g EPA/DHA daily — anti-inflammatory, fat oxidation, fat storage inhibition. Flaxseed oil conversion to EPA/DHA is "exceedingly inefficient" — not a substitute.

---

### Training During Cut vs. Bulk

**Core principle:** Heavy intensity is non-negotiable; volume can be cut by up to two-thirds. Keep the weight on the bar the same — if it drops, muscle loss is happening.

**Frequency:** 1.5–2x per week per muscle group (upper/lower split, 4 days/week). Same for both bulking and cutting. Reducing frequency during a cut is a common mistake.

**Volume per session:** 8–10 hard sets per muscle group is sufficient. More is not better, especially in a deficit.

**Cardio during cut:** Low-intensity steady-state (30+ min) preferred over HIIT when doing resistance training + caloric restriction simultaneously. Adding HIIT on top is the cortisol-stacking trap.

**Age 40+ trainees:** Err on the low side of volume and frequency. Recovery capacity declines significantly. Hormonal decline reduces anabolic response to training. Margin for overtraining shrinks.

### Refeeds

**Mechanism:** Leptin drops 30–50% within the first week of dieting (before significant fat is lost), triggering reduced appetite suppression, lower metabolic rate, increased cortisol. Only dietary carbohydrate raises leptin in the 2–72 hour window — fat overfeeding does not. Refeeds interrupt this adaptation.

**Duration:** 24–48 hours provides complete hormonal restoration (even 5 hours raises leptin measurably).

**Frequency by body fat (men):**
- >25% BF: every 2–4 weeks
- 15–25% BF: every 1–2 weeks
- <15% BF: 1–2x per week minimum
- <10% BF: more frequent and/or larger refeeds required

**Macros during refeed:** Carbohydrates are the only lever. Fat stays low (same or lower than normal diet). Protein stays the same.

**Cheat day ≠ refeed:** A cheat day loaded with fat and protein has minimal leptin impact. A refeed is specifically high-carb, low-fat. These are physiologically distinct.

### Diet Breaks

**Duration:** 7–14 days (prefers 10–14 for full hormonal normalization — thyroid requires at least 7 days to begin normalizing).

**Calories:** Maintenance (14–16 cal/lb for men). Must reach 120–150g carbs/day minimum for thyroid recovery.

**Why it works:**
- Restores leptin and thyroid T3 conversion
- Reduces cortisol → releases retained water
- Normalizes testosterone suppression from dieting
- Restores glycogen and training capacity
- Psychological: breaks a long diet into manageable chunks

**Frequency:** Every 4–6 weeks for lean individuals (<15% BF); every 6–12 weeks for moderate BF.

**The Long-Term Delayed Fat Loss Effect (LTDFLE):** After extended dieting, raising calories to maintenance triggers 4–7 days of continued fat loss — cortisol-driven water loss, glycogen fullness, leptin-driven fat oxidation, thyroid normalization. Competitive athletes can time this deliberately: end the hard diet 5–7 days before an event and let the LTDFLE do final peaking.

### Body Recomposition

**Who can do it:** Beginners (newbie gains), returning trainees (muscle memory), obese individuals (high BF = favorable P-ratio), enhanced athletes (PEDs override partitioning limits).

**Who cannot meaningfully recomp:** Trained natural athletes already lean. After 2+ years of lifting at 10–15% BF, simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain produces negligible results in either direction.

**P-ratio:** Starting body fat % predicts how calories partition. Lean individuals lose more LBM when cutting. Those who dieted down have worse P-ratios than naturally lean individuals — their bodies are primed to restore fat first upon refeeding.

**Practical path:** Diet to 10–12% BF → 2 weeks at maintenance → bulk to 15% BF → diet back down. Cycle repeatedly.

### Supplements

**Worth using:**
- Fish oil: 1.8–3.0 g EPA/DHA/day
- Vitamin D: 2,000 IU/day minimum; target 50 ng/ml serum
- Creatine monohydrate: 3–5 g/day; expect 2–4 lb water weight gain
- Beta-alanine: 1.6–3.2 g/day split into 4 doses (for 30–60 sec high-intensity efforts)
- Multivitamin: nutritional insurance
- Zinc (25 mg) + Magnesium (400–500 mg) at bedtime for sleep and recovery

**Not worth it:** High-dose antioxidants during training (block adaptation signaling — counterproductive), glutamine, protein hydrolysates, flaxseed oil as EPA/DHA source.

**GLP-1 drugs:** Lyle wrote a dedicated book (The GLP-1 Solution) covering tirzepatide specifically. Key themes: aggressively prioritize protein (appetite suppression causes under-eating protein), resistance training 3x/week is mandatory to prevent lean mass loss, the normal rules on deficit and loss rate shift with pharmacological assistance.

**HGH:** At therapeutic doses, works primarily through lipolysis/fat oxidation and connective tissue health — not direct muscle building. In combination with insulin + testosterone at bodybuilder doses, the synergy changes; but HGH alone (or with TRT) is not a significant muscle-building tool.

### Genetic Potential and Realistic Expectations

**Annual muscle gain for natural males:**
- Year 1: 10–12 lbs
- Year 2: 5–6 lbs
- Year 3: 2–3 lbs
- Beyond Year 3: ~1 lb/year

**Age 40+:** Lower ceiling, lower recovery, reduced hormonal environment. TRT at 150–200 mg/week is therapeutic range; above this is pharmacological enhancement.

## Where Lyle Differs / Contradicts

### 1. Late-prep protein reduction — MOST IMPORTANT DIVERGENCE
**PDF says:** Drop protein to 1g/lb late prep, redistribute as carbs for gym performance.
**Lyle says:** The exact opposite. As you get leaner, protein requirements increase (up to 1.5 g/lb LBM at single-digit BF). Late prep is the worst time to cut protein — muscle catabolism risk is highest when leanest. Better solution: keep protein at 1.4–1.5 g/lb LBM and find carb calories by cutting fat slightly.

### 2. Per-meal protein ceiling
**PDF says:** 43–54g per meal maxes MPS.
**Lyle says:** MPS-based ceiling ignores 70% of what protein does. No hard ceiling in real-world conditions. (Practical impact is small at 215g ÷ 4 meals = ~54g/meal.)

### 3. Early fat loss rate
**PDF says:** 1.5% BW/week is appropriate early in prep (≈2.7 lbs/week at 179 lbs).
**Lyle says:** 1–1.5 lbs/week is optimal for muscle retention. 2–3 lbs/week is usable in 2–4 week bursts only. Sustained rapid loss increases muscle loss risk. (Note: Tirzepatide may change this calculus.)

### 4. HGH as a muscle-building tool
**PDF says:** HGH is a key body composition tool.
**Lyle says:** Evidence shows therapeutic HGH alone doesn't significantly build muscle — works primarily through lipolysis and connective tissue. Don't rely on it for muscle retention; protein + resistance training do that work.

### 5. Cardio stacking during prep
**PDF implies:** More cardio is a standard deficit tool.
**Lyle says:** This is the cortisol trap. On Tirzepatide (which already creates a large pharmacological deficit), adding heavy cardio risks chronic cortisol elevation, water retention masking progress, and metabolic suppression.

## What the PDFs Miss That Lyle Covers

**The leptin mechanism:** Only carbs raise leptin short-term. Fat-heavy cheat meals accomplish nothing as refeeds. Refeed frequency must increase as BF drops.

**LTDFLE:** After extended dieting, raising calories to maintenance triggers 4–7 days of continued fat loss. Time this deliberately for visual peak.

**Cortisol paradox:** Large deficit + large cardio is not additive — it's counterproductive. Especially critical with Tirzepatide suppressing intake.

**Connective tissue protein:** Tendons, ligaments, cartilage all need protein synthesis. MPS studies miss this. More important at 45+.

**P-ratio:** BF% determines calorie partitioning. Explains why the bulk-and-cut cycle works better than recomping.

**Flexible dieting psychology:** Rigid protocols cause worse long-term outcomes than flexible, planned approaches. Scheduled diet breaks improve adherence.

**Age-specific volume:** PDFs don't stratify by age. At 45, err on low side of volume and frequency.

**GLP-1 integration:** PDFs predate this entirely. On Tirzepatide, protein must be actively tracked, resistance training is non-negotiable, and the normal deficit/loss-rate rules shift.

## Reconciled Protocol for Eric
*45yo, 179 lbs, ~15% BF, Tirzepatide + HGH, cutting to 167 lbs / ~10% BF, then lean bulk*

**Protein:** Hold at 210–225g throughout the cut. Do NOT reduce late in prep — if anything, push to 220–228g as BF drops. On Tirzepatide, actively track protein per meal; appetite suppression makes it easy to miss daily targets.

**Deficit:** Tirzepatide is generating significant pharmacological deficit. Aim for 1–1.5 lbs/week. Do not stack heavy cardio on top — the drug is already doing that work. Modest cardio (30 min low-intensity, 3–4x/week) for health/NEAT only.

**Calories:** The PDF's 2,100 cal target is at the aggressive end of Lyle's moderate deficit range for 179 lbs. On Tirzepatide, hitting 2,100 may require conscious effort. Prioritize protein first, then fill with carbs on training days.

**Refeeds:** At 15% → 10% BF trajectory, increase to 1–2x/week as leanness increases. Carbs to 200–300g on refeed days; fat stays low (≤50g); protein unchanged. Not a cheat day.

**Diet break:** Mandatory, not optional. Schedule one 10–14 day break at the midpoint of the cut. Reach 120–150g carbs/day minimum. At 45, the hormonal reset value is amplified.

**Training:** Upper/lower 4 days/week. 8–10 hard sets per muscle group per session. Keep weight on the bar — dropping weight is the muscle loss signal, not the scale. Don't stack HIIT with lifting.

**HGH expectation:** Supports fat oxidation and connective tissue — not a muscle-retention tool. Protein + resistance training carry that.

**LTDFLE:** After the full cut, raise to maintenance for 4–7 days before final photos/measurements to capture the delayed fat loss effect and look the best at the leanest point.

**Post-cut transition:** 2–4 weeks at maintenance (~2,500–2,700 cal at 167 lbs) before starting lean bulk. Let testosterone, leptin, and thyroid normalize first.

**Lean bulk:** 167 lbs × 16–18 cal/lb = 2,670–3,000 cal. Bulk to ~175–178 lbs (~15% BF), then cut again. On Tirzepatide, hitting a caloric surplus will require intentional effort — the drug will suppress the hunger that normally drives a bulk.
